01The Four Tools

Each tool does one main job. The rod is the frame you hold and cast with; the reel mounts on the rod and stores and releases the line; the landing net lifts a hooked fish safely out of the water. Match the right tool to the fish and you are ready to fish almost anywhere in Ogasawara — from the high rocky shore to deep offshore water.

02Fishing Rods

The rod is your main tool and the frame the whole setup is built on. The reel attaches to it, the line runs along it, and its length and bend give you the power to cast far and to fight a fish back in.

The main parts

  • Tip — the thin top end of the rod
  • Blank — the main body
  • Guides — the rings the line runs through
  • Reel seat — where the reel clamps on
  • Handle / grip — where you hold it

How to set up and use a rod

  1. Assemble the rod. Join the sections from the tip down, and line up all the guides in one straight row.
  2. Attach the reel. Slide the reel foot into the reel seat and tighten the locking ring so the reel cannot move.
  3. Thread the line. Pass the line through every guide, starting at the largest guide near the reel and working up to the tip. Do not skip any.
  4. Tie on your tackle. Attach the hook, lure, jig, or sinker with a strong knot (see the knots table on the last page).
  5. Cast. Hold the rod firmly, release the line, and swing the rod smoothly forward toward your target.
  6. Retrieve. Wind the reel handle at a steady speed, faster or slower depending on the fish.

03Spinning Reels

The spinning reel mounts underneath the rod. It is the easiest reel to learn, casts light lures well, and rarely tangles — which makes it the best choice for shore fishing.

How-to videoyoutube.com/watch?v=C7CWIywYmxk

How to use a spinning reel

  1. Open the bail. Flip the metal bail arm up so the spool can release line.
  2. Hold the line. Trap the line against the rod with your index finger so nothing slips out yet.
  3. Swing and cast. Bring the rod back over your shoulder, then sweep it smoothly forward toward your target.
  4. Release your finger. As the rod points forward, lift your index finger so the line shoots off the spool.
  5. Close the bail. Once the lure lands, flip the bail down by hand and wind once to tighten the line.
  6. Fight the fish. Keep the rod tip up and let the reel's drag give line to a strong fish — do not force it.

Tip: always flip the bail closed by hand rather than winding it shut — this prevents line twist and tangles.

A 5000–6000 reel with PE 2–4 braided line and a 30–60 lb leader handles most Ogasawara fishing. Step up to 8000–14000 for giant trevally and tuna.

04Bait (Baitcasting) Reels

The baitcasting reel mounts on top of the rod. It is more advanced than a spinning reel, but gives more power, more accuracy, and a stronger drag — which makes it ideal for the big, hard-pulling fish found around Ogasawara.

How to use a baitcaster

  1. Set the spool tension. Press the thumb bar and turn the tension knob until the lure drops slowly and the spool stops on its own when it lands.
  2. Set the brakes. Start with the brakes high (around 70–80%), then lower them one step at a time as you get more comfortable.
  3. Press the thumb bar. Hold the spool gently with your thumb so it cannot move until you cast.
  4. Cast. Swing the rod forward and ease your thumb off the spool to let the lure fly.
  5. Feather and stop. Lightly touch the spool with your thumb in flight, and press down just before the lure hits the water.
  6. Retrieve. Turn the handle — the reel re-engages automatically — and wind in.

Avoiding the "bird's nest" (backlash): this tangle happens when the spool spins faster than the line leaves. Prevent it with steady thumb control, higher brakes, and heavier lures while you learn. If it tangles, increase the brakes one step.

05The Telescopic Landing Net

This is a long, extendable pole with a net basket on the end — not a thrown cast net. You extend the handle to reach down to the water, then scoop a hooked fish into the net. It is essential in Ogasawara, where many fishing spots sit on high rock ledges well above the sea, and it is just as useful from a boat.

The main parts

  • Telescopic handle — slides out and locks to an adjustable length
  • Locking collars / joints — hold each section at the chosen length
  • Net head (hoop and mesh bag) — screws onto the tip of the handle
  • Shoulder strap — for carrying the collapsed pole

How to set up and use it

  1. Fit the net head. Screw or clip the net hoop onto the threaded tip of the handle before you start fishing.
  2. Extend to length. Pull out the sections (or twist the locking collar to release it), set the length you need to reach the water, then lock each joint firmly.
  3. Keep it within reach. Rest the net beside you, but do not extend it fully until a fish is hooked and coming close.
  4. Tire the fish first. Fight the fish until it stops pulling hard — trying to net a green, fighting fish usually loses it.
  5. Net it head-first. Lower the hoop into the water and lead the fish in head-first. Never chase its tail with the net.
  6. Lift straight up. Once the fish is in the bag, lift smoothly and draw the pole up. Collapse the sections afterward for easy carrying.

Practical tips

  • A rubberised, non-slip grip holds well even with wet hands or gloves.
  • Match the length to your spot — Ogasawara's rock ledges are high, so a long handle (around 5–6 m) is common.
  • Rinse the pole and joints with fresh water after use to stop salt seizing the locking collars.
  • Best for landing snapper, grouper, trevally, and barracuda — any fish too large or too high up to lift safely by the line.
